Navigating Protests Under the Racing Rules of sailing
Author: John Golding

An online assistant for new racing sailors (youth and adults) to help you understand what happens before, during, and after a protest under the Racing Rules of Sailing, without changing any official wording or procedures from the rule book or related documents. The assistant guides you through what is expected of you, what you must do yourself, and what the protest committee will do at each stage.
What This Assistant Does
Explains the overall protest and redress process so you know what to expect, from the incident on the water to the end of any hearing, reopening, or appeal (where applicable).
Keeps all official terminology and procedures exactly as they appear in the Racing Rules of Sailing and associated documents, to avoid confusion or misinterpretation.
Breaks the process into clear sections written at about a Grade 12 reading level, so first‑time racers and parents can follow along without legal or technical training.
The assistant does not replace the rule book or any official notices of race, sailing instructions, or national authority procedures; it helps you navigate and apply them more confidently

Using the Assistant as a Protestor
You can follow step‑by‑step guidance starting just before and immediately after an incident on the water. The assistant walks you through:
On the water: When and how to hail, what you must do to make a valid protest, and what notifications are required under the Racing Rules of Sailing.
Protest form: The minimum information that must be included on a protest or request for redress form, and what details are optional or not required for the protest to be valid.
On shore: How and where to submit the protest or request for redress, what time limits apply, and what will typically happen during the hearing.

After the decision: How to read the decision, when a reopening may be possible, and when an appeal or other procedure is available under the relevant rules and any “no appeal” conditions in the sailing instructions or notice of race.
Each of these stages is presented in its own section so you can jump directly to “On the water,” “Protest form,” “Hearing,” “Reopening,” “Appeals,” or “Redress” as needed.

Using the Assistant as a Protestee
If another boat protests you, the assistant helps you understand your options from the moment of the incident. It provides sections covering:
On the water: What to consider just before and after the possible foul, including when you may take a penalty on the water and when a percentage penalty or other penalty may apply according to the rules and event documents.
Preparing on shore: How to get ready for the hearing, what information you should bring, and what you can expect from the protest committee.
During and after the hearing: How decisions are written, when and how a hearing might be reopened, and your rights and responsibilities as a party.
You can also review dedicated sections on appeals and requests for redress so you understand when they are appropriate and how they work from the protestee’s point of view.

Appeals and Requests for Redress
The assistant includes separate, plain‑language sections on:
Appeals: When an appeal may be available under the rules, what time limits and procedures apply, and how “no appeal” provisions (for example, certain qualifiers) can change your options.
Requests for redress: When redress may be appropriate, how to file a request, and what you should be prepared to show in the hearing.
Reopening: When a protest committee may reopen a hearing, and when and how a party can request a reopening, including any special procedures described in championship or qualifier documents.
These sections carefully follow the language and structure of the Racing Rules of Sailing and official guidance, to avoid paraphrasing or altering any rules
