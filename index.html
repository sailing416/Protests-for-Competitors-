Navigating Protests
A guide for new competitors and coaches
⚓
This guide is for new racing sailors and coaches at all levels. It walks you through the protest process step by step — on the water and on shore. All content is drawn directly from the Racing Rules of Sailing 2025–2028 (RRS), the World Sailing Case Book 2021–2024, the Judges' Manual 2021, and Sail Canada Prescriptions 2025–2028. No alternative wording has been introduced.

📋 What to Expect

A protest is a formal complaint that a racing rule was broken. There are specific steps required on the water and on shore. Missing any of them can mean your protest is not valid — or that you lose rights you would otherwise have.

The process has two phases: on the water (during or right after the incident) and on shore (filing, hearings, and any appeals). The protest process is not about punishment — it is about applying the rules fairly. Hearings are conducted calmly and with structure. You will have a chance to tell your side, call witnesses, and ask questions.

🚩 Protestor — On the Water
What to do at and immediately after the incident
Hailing “Protest” Rule 61.1(a)

When you believe another boat has broken a rule, you must hail “Protest” as soon as reasonably possible after the incident. This is required. If you do not hail, your protest will almost certainly be ruled invalid at the hearing.

Displaying a Red Flag Rule 61.1(a)

At the same time, display a red flag conspicuously and keep it flying until you finish or retire. In fleet racing the flag must be shown before or when you finish.

✓
Exception — small boats: If your boat is less than 6 metres (about 20 feet) in overall length, no flag is required. You must still hail “Protest.”
Informing the Other Boat Rule 61.1(a)

When you know the identity of the boat you intend to protest, try to inform her at the first reasonable opportunity. You are not required to succeed — but you must genuinely try. If she is too far away to hear, note that and carry on.

  • Hail by sail number or name: “Protest, [sail number]!”
  • If you could not identify the boat, record hull colour, class, and sail markings to describe her on the form.
  • You may also inform her on shore before the filing deadline.
⚠
Critical: If you do not hail “Protest” at the time of the incident, the protest committee will ask. If you cannot confirm a hail was made, your protest will not proceed.
  1. Incident occursImmediately hail “Protest” loudly.
  2. Display red flagHold it high and clearly visible. Keep it flying until you finish.
  3. Inform the other boatHail her sail number. If she is out of earshot, try again as soon as possible.
  4. Note all detailsTime, location on course, positions of both boats, what happened. Write it down as soon as you finish.
  5. File your protest within the time limitCheck the NOR and sailing instructions to find out how to file — many events now require online submission (e.g. yachtscoring.com) rather than a physical race office. Rule 60.3(b) permits delivery “by such other method as stated in the sailing instructions.”
📝 Protestor — Filing the Form
What must be on the form, the deadline, and what is not required
⚠
Time Limit — Critical Rule 60.3(b)
Rule 60.3(b) states: “A protest shall be delivered to the race office (or by such other method as stated in the sailing instructions) within the protest time limit.” The default time limit is two hours after the last boat in the race finishes. However, if the sailing instructions state a different time limit, that time limit applies instead.

📄 Read the NOR and Sailing Instructions Before Racing. Many regattas — including most major Canadian events — now require protests to be filed online through platforms such as yachtscoring.com or another regatta management system, rather than at a physical race office. The sailing instructions will specify the required method. If you go to a race office that no longer exists or use the wrong method, your protest may be invalid. Check before you race.

Under the Sail Canada Prescription to Part 5: “No fee shall be charged for any procedure of the protest committee under Part 5 or Appendix T.”

What MUST Be on the Form Rule 60.3(a)

  • ✅Identity (sail number or name) of the boat being protested
  • ✅Identity of your boat (the protesting boat)
  • ✅Date, time, and place of the incident
  • ✅A brief description of what happened
  • ✅The rule or rules you believe were broken (e.g. Rule 11 — On the Same Tack, Overlapped)
  • ✅Name and signature of a representative of your boat

What is NOT Required

  • ⚠You do not need to prove your case on the form — just describe what happened clearly.
  • ⚠You do not need a perfect diagram. A rough sketch is fine. The form can be amended before the hearing.
  • ⚠You do not need to know the exact rule number — approximate identification is acceptable.
  • ⚠No filing fee can be charged under Sail Canada Prescriptions.
📝
Rule 63.3   A protest that does not comply with rule 61 shall be ruled invalid. However, the protest committee may extend the time limit or permit amendment of a defective protest if there is good reason to do so. If in doubt, file and let the committee decide.
⚖ Protestor — The Hearing
What happens in the hearing room and your role
Notice and Your Right to Attend Rule 63.2

The protest committee must give all parties reasonable notice of the hearing time and place. Both boats have the right to attend, present their case, call witnesses, and question the other party's witnesses.

Order of Events

  1. Validity checkThe committee reads the form and confirms the protest is valid.
  2. Protestor presentsYou tell your version of the incident. Stick to what you directly observed. Bring your diagram and any notes.
  3. Protestee presentsThe other boat tells their version.
  4. WitnessesBoth sides may call witnesses. All witnesses may be questioned by both parties and the committee.
  5. Parties leaveBoth boats leave the room while the committee deliberates.
  6. Decision announcedBoth parties are recalled. The decision is announced and must be given in writing on request.
⚖
Standard of Proof: The committee decides facts “on the balance of probabilities” — what most likely happened based on the evidence. You do not need to prove beyond a reasonable doubt.
Tips for Protestors in the Room
  • Bring your diagram and notes made immediately after the race.
  • Speak clearly and describe only what you directly observed.
  • If you have a crew member who saw the incident, bring them as a witness.
  • Be respectful of the committee. They are qualified volunteers applying the rules fairly.
🛡 Protestee — On the Water
Your options when another boat hails “Protest”
Stay Calm — Note Everything

If another boat hails “Protest” and displays a red flag, note the time, location on the course, which boats were involved, and exactly what each boat was doing. Your memory of these details is your most valuable evidence.

📄
Read the NOR and Sailing Instructions Before Racing — Penalties Can Change
The Notice of Race (NOR) and Sailing Instructions (SIs) may modify the standard penalty system. The number of turns, the type of penalty, or the entire system may be different from event to event. You are responsible for knowing which system applies before the start. Rule 86.1 permits these changes. Do not assume the default rules apply without checking.

Which Penalty System Applies? Rule 44 & Appendix P

The Racing Rules of Sailing set a default, but the NOR or SIs for your event may change it. Three systems are commonly used:

🔴 Two-Turns Penalty (Default) Rule 44.2

The standard penalty when no change is specified in the sailing instructions. After breaking a rule of Part 2, take two full 360° circles — each circle includes one tack and one gybe — as soon as possible after the incident, clear of other boats.

🔵 One-Turn Penalty Rule 44.1 / SI change

Some events — particularly short-course or youth events — specify a One-Turn Penalty in the sailing instructions. If so, one full 360° turn (one tack and one gybe) is sufficient. Only valid if stated in the SIs.

🟡 Scoring (Percentage) Penalty Appendix P

Some events use a Scoring Penalty instead of turns, when specified in the notice of race or sailing instructions. Rule 44.3(a) states: “a boat takes a Scoring Penalty by displaying a yellow flag at the first reasonable opportunity after the incident.” She keeps the yellow flag displayed until finishing and calls the race committee's attention to it at the finishing line. Her score is worsened by the number of points stated in the NOR or SIs. Only valid if specified in the NOR or SIs. Always verify the required flag — do not assume.

How to Take the Penalty (Turns)

  1. Move clear immediatelyGet away from other boats right after the incident.
  2. Complete the required turnsOne or two full 360° circles as required — each includes one tack and one gybe. Do this as soon as possible, not at the next mark or finish.
  3. Resume racingOnce the penalty is completed correctly, you are exonerated and the protest cannot proceed for that incident.

Option — Defend at the Hearing

If you believe you did not break a rule, you are not required to take any penalty. Carry on racing normally. The other boat will file a protest on shore, and you will have a full hearing to tell your side.

⚖ Protestee — The Hearing
What to expect and how to present your case
You Will Receive Notice Rule 63.2

The race office will notify you that a protest has been filed against you, and tell you when and where the hearing will be held. You have the right to attend. Do not miss it — if you fail to attend without good reason, the committee may proceed without you.

What to Bring

  • ✅Your own notes or diagram of the incident, made as soon as possible after the race.
  • ✅Witnesses — crew members or other boats who saw what happened.
  • ✅A calm, clear account of what you saw and where each boat was.
⚠
Rule 64.1(a)   When the committee finds a boat has broken a rule and is not exonerated, it shall disqualify her unless some other penalty applies. You have the right to receive the decision in writing and the right to appeal.
Tips for Protestees in the Room
  • You are not there to argue with the other boat — you are presenting facts to the committee.
  • Be respectful. Hearings are run by qualified volunteers doing their best to apply the rules fairly.
  • Describe only what you directly observed. Do not guess at what the other boat was doing.
  • If you disagree with the outcome, you have full appeal rights.
📨 Appeals
Reviewing a protest committee decision — when and how
📨
Rule 70.1   A party to a protest hearing may appeal a decision of the protest committee, except when the sailing instructions have excluded the right to appeal or an international jury has been appointed.
✓ When You CAN Appeal
  • The committee applied a rule incorrectly to the facts they found.
  • The procedure was improper — e.g. you were not given a fair chance to be heard.
✕ When You CANNOT Appeal
  • You disagree with the findings of fact (those cannot be appealed).
  • The sailing instructions state decisions are final (no-appeal event).
  • An international jury was appointed.

How to Appeal — Sail Canada

Appeals are submitted in writing by email to: appeals [at] sailing.ca

Your appeal must include:

  • ✅A copy of the protest form
  • ✅The written decision of the protest committee
  • ✅A written statement explaining which rule you believe was incorrectly applied and why
✓
Sail Canada Prescription — No Fee: No fee shall be charged for any procedure of the protest committee under Part 5 or Appendix T, including appeals. Sail Canada may delegate to a provincial appeals committee. Decisions of a provincial appeals committee may be further appealed to Sail Canada.
🔄 Request for Redress
Correcting an unfair score through no fault of your own
🔄
Rule 62.1   A boat may request redress if her score has been, or may be, through no fault of her own, made significantly worse by: (a) an improper action or omission of the race committee, protest committee, organizing authority, or measurement committee; (b) injury or physical damage because of the action of a boat that was breaking a rule of Part 2 or of a vessel not racing; (c) giving help to a person or vessel in danger; or (d) a boat against which a penalty has been imposed under rule 2 or rule 69.2(b)(ii).
🚩 Protest vs. Redress

A protest is used when another boat broke a rule against you during racing.

A request for redress is used when your score was harmed by race management error, accident, or rescue — not a rule breach by another competitor.

Common Grounds for Redress
  • Race committee gave an incorrect signal or shortened course improperly
  • You were physically damaged by a boat responding to an emergency
  • You stopped to help a person or vessel in danger

How to Request Redress

  1. File in writingSubmit a written request for redress within the same time limit as a protest. Check the NOR and sailing instructions for the required method of delivery — many events now require online filing rather than a physical race office (Rule 60.3(b): “by such other method as stated in the sailing instructions”).
  2. Describe the harmExplain what happened and how your score was significantly worsened through no fault of your own.
  3. A hearing is heldThe protest committee will hold a hearing using the same procedure as a protest hearing.
  4. Score adjusted if grantedIf redress is granted, the committee will adjust your score in a way that is equitable to all boats in the race or series.
💡
Any boat — whether protestor or protestee in a separate hearing — may separately request redress if the grounds under rule 62.1 apply. A request for redress is its own independent proceeding.
